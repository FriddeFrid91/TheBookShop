In The Time of Contempt, political chaos erupts as a mage coup forces Ciri to flee to the desert, where she discovers her resilience and powers. The Shining

Stephen King

In The Shining by Stephen King, Jack Torrance becomes the caretaker of the haunted Overlook Hotel, where supernatural forces and isolation drive him mad. His psychic son, Danny, uncovers the hotel’s dark secrets, and he and his mother must fight to survive Jack’s descent into violence.

Stephen King

It by Stephen King follows the Losers' Club as they battle Pennywise, a shape-shifting entity that preys on children in Derry every 27 years. As kids and later as adults, they confront their fears to defeat the monster, highlighting themes of friendship and courage.

Science Fiction

Neuromancer

William Gibson

Neuromancer by William Gibson follows Case, a washed-up hacker, hired for a final job by a mysterious employer, Armitage, and a skilled mercenary, Molly. Together, they navigate a dystopian cyberpunk world to hack into a powerful AI. The novel explores themes of technology, identity, and humanity in a digitized future.

Mona Lisa Overdrive

William Gibson

Mona Lisa Overdrive by William Gibson is the final book in the Sprawl trilogy, intertwining four narratives: Angie, a famous simstim star connected to cyberspace; Mona, a street girl drawn into a conspiracy; Slick, a mechanic aiding a mysterious AI; and Kumiko, the daughter of a Yakuza boss seeking refuge. Their paths converge as an AI seeks transcendence, exploring themes of identity, technology, and humanity's relationship with artificial intelligence.

Count Zero

William Gibson

Count Zero by William Gibson is a cyberpunk novel set in the same world as Neuromancer. It follows three interwoven stories: Turner, a mercenary hired to extract a corporate defector; Bobby, a novice hacker caught in a deadly conspiracy; and Marly, an art dealer investigating mysterious AI-created works. Their journeys converge, exploring themes of corporate power, artificial intelligence, and human connection.
